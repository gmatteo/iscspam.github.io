From Structure to Function: Designing Materials with Desired Properties through Structure-Property Insights

Computational materials design has emerged as a pivotal approach for accelerating the development of new materials, addressing both current and future technological challenges. By understanding the fundamental nature of structure-property relationships, we can develop materials that combine all desirable features while avoiding unwanted ones.
In this talk, I will focus on two classes of materials: organic-inorganic halide perovskites and molecular crystals. Halide perovskites have garnered significant attention in recent years due to their exceptional photovoltaic performance, attributed to properties such as tunable band gaps, low exciton binding energy, and long charge carrier recombination lifetimes. Despite their high power conversion efficiency, questions remain regarding their structural, electronic and optical properties, particularly concerning long-term stability. Through theoretical analyses, we propose design principles aimed at synthesizing stable halide perovskites, making them promising candidates for solar cell applications [1-3].
Similarly, molecular crystals are extensively used as active pharmaceutical ingredients and in other sectors, such as food, agriculture, and polymers. A major challenge in these industries, particularly pharmaceuticals, is the existence of multiple crystal forms (polymorphs) for the same compound. The physico-chemical properties, including thermal stability, solubility and dissolution rate, can dramatically vary between polymorphs, significatnly impacting the drug efficacy. Thus, accurately predicting the correct polymorph of a compound is a critical goal.
THz spectroscopy is valuable in this endeavor, as each polymorph's unique spectral fingerprint aids in their identification. However, deciphering the vibrational spectra of molecular crystals poses a significant challenge. Assigning peaks to specific vibrational modes typically requires experience and chemical intuition, but the complexity of these spectra often makes such assignments impractical. Quantum chemistry methods have proven reliable in accurately simulating the vibrational spectra.
In this context, I will introduce TheSeuSS, an automated computational platform that efficiently simulates IR, Raman and THz spectra for both periodic and non-periodic systems at DFT and DFTB levels of theory [4]. TheSeuSS also provides an accurate description of intermolecular forces, crucial for molecular crystals as their molecules are held together by weak intermolecular forces. Our user-friendly platform allows users with varying expertise to perform THz spectra simulations, facilitating efficient and accurate high-throughput screening of multiple polymorphs, significantly contributing to the broader efforts in crystal structure prediction studies. By drawing comparisons and highlighting the synergies between these two areas of research, we demonstrate how a fundamental understanding of structure-property relationships can lead to innovative design principles, tailored for specific applications.
