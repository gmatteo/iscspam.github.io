Intelligent Design of Electron-Rich Materials for Catalysis and Energy Applications

Electron-rich intermetallic compounds, such as electrides and MAX/MAB phases, possess several key characteristics including low work function, strong electron-donating ability, and strong inert bonding force. These compounds have shown great potential in various catalytic reactions, such as ammonia synthesis, ammonia decomposition, CO2 hydrogenation, and alkali metal ion batteries. As a result, they have attracted significant research interest. However, electron-rich materials exhibit wide compositional variation and diverse structures, which presents a challenge in efficiently designing new multi-element compounds of this type.
Conventional approaches to material design and development, which rely on trial and error based on experience and experiments, are inefficient and time-consuming. These methods fall short of meeting the urgent demands for new electron-rich materials in core applications. To address this issue, recent exploratory studies have focused on employing materials genetic engineering techniques, such as high-throughput computational screening, structure prediction, and machine learning, to design and develop novel electron-rich materials more efficiently. By using high-throughput computational screening, researchers can rapidly evaluate a large number of potential compound configurations and identify promising candidates for further investigation (Figure 1). Structure prediction techniques help in predicting the atomic arrangements and crystal structures of these compounds. Additionally, machine learning algorithms can be employed to analyze the vast amount of existing data and establish relationships between material properties and composition, aiding in the identification of optimal chemical combinations for targeted properties. The speaker will present the results of recent research efforts that utilize these materials genetic engineering methods for the design and development of novel electron-rich materials. These advancements offer a promising avenue for accelerating the discovery process and addressing the urgent need for efficient design approaches in this field.
